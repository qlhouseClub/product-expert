# Commercialization and Growth

Load this module for market selection, business-model design, pricing, packaging, go-to-market, acquisition, expansion, or growth-loop work. Use it with the shared evidence ledger; commercial polish must not turn assumptions into facts.

## Choose the commercial decision

Name the decision before choosing a framework:

- Which market or segment should receive investment?
- Who uses, benefits, decides, approves, pays, implements, and renews?
- What value is created, how is it measured, and how can the business capture part of it?
- What price metric and package boundaries align payment with value and cost?
- What route to market can reach the buyer with acceptable economics and operational load?
- Which growth constraint should be addressed now?

Do not produce a full commercial plan when one decision is sufficient.

## Build the commercial system

Map the connected system rather than a funnel in isolation:

| Element | Questions |
|---|---|
| Segment | What shared situation, need, behavior, economics, and buying process make this group actionable? |
| Roles | Who is the user, beneficiary, champion, buyer, approver, blocker, administrator, and renewal owner? |
| Alternatives | What do they use today, including manual work, services, delay, and doing nothing? |
| Value | What revenue, cost, time, risk, quality, status, or strategic option changes? |
| Capture | Subscription, usage, transaction, service, license, outcome share, cross-sell, or cost reduction? |
| Distribution | Self-serve, sales-led, partner, marketplace, community, embedded, procurement, or hybrid? |
| Operations | Onboarding, implementation, support, success, billing, compliance, and renewal burden? |
| Economics | Price, volume, gross margin, cost-to-serve, acquisition cost, payback, retention, and cash timing? |

Use observed behavior, contract history, win/loss evidence, support data, and sales calls before survey intent where possible.

## Market and segment selection

1. Define the market around a job and buying context, not a vague category label.
2. Estimate size with a transparent bottom-up model when decision quality requires it. Show variables, ranges, sources, and sensitivity.
3. Assess urgency, frequency, current spend, switching friction, reachability, sales cycle, regulatory burden, competitive intensity, and strategic fit.
4. Select a beachhead only when the segment is narrow enough to serve coherently and large or strategic enough to justify entry.
5. State adjacent segments and the evidence required before expansion.

Avoid precise TAM/SAM/SOM figures based only on third-party market reports.

## Pricing and packaging

### Pricing evidence ladder

Prefer, in descending order where available:

1. Paid behavior, renewals, expansion, discounts, churn, and procurement outcomes
2. Comparable transactions and win/loss evidence
3. Controlled offer, package, or price tests
4. Structured research such as choice tasks, willingness-to-pay interviews, or price-sensitivity surveys
5. Expert judgment and benchmarks, explicitly labeled as provisional

### Design sequence

1. Define the value metric: the unit that best tracks customer value without creating perverse behavior.
2. Establish economic constraints: variable cost, support load, channel margin, cash timing, and minimum sustainable contribution.
3. Identify meaningful differentiation dimensions: capability, capacity, service, control, compliance, performance, or outcome.
4. Create packages around coherent jobs or customer maturity, not arbitrary feature withholding.
5. Model price-volume, mix, discount, expansion, churn, gross-margin, and cash-flow sensitivity.
6. Test comprehension, fairness, upgrade logic, billing predictability, and operational feasibility.
7. For a price change, define grandfathering, notice, migration, exception authority, measurement, and rollback.

Never treat a single ratio, competitor price, or willingness-to-pay answer as the price.

## Go-to-market design

Define:

- ICP and exclusion criteria
- Buying committee, decision process, procurement, security, and legal path
- Positioning: category/context, alternative, differentiated mechanism, proof, and reason to believe
- Motion: self-serve, sales-assisted, enterprise sales, partner, or hybrid, chosen from buying complexity and economics
- Channel thesis, acquisition mechanism, cost, control, feedback speed, and saturation risk
- Sales stages, exit evidence, enablement, demo/proof, objections, and handoff
- Activation, implementation, onboarding, support, customer success, renewal, and expansion
- Launch sequence, cohort, message, owner, readiness, guardrails, and stop/expand rules

Do not confuse launch communications with distribution. A channel is useful only if it can repeatedly reach qualified demand.

## Growth diagnosis

1. Define the constrained outcome and segment.
2. Build a causal model across acquisition quality, activation, repeated value, retention, monetization, expansion, and referral.
3. Locate the binding constraint with evidence; do not optimize every stage simultaneously.
4. Generate levers that change the mechanism, not only interface copy.
5. Distinguish a one-time campaign from a durable loop. For each loop, define entry, value creation, output, reinvestment, friction, and saturation.
6. Prioritize experiments by expected information value, customer value, business leverage, integrity, effort, and reversibility.
7. Protect trust, user agency, support load, unit economics, and long-term retention with guardrails.

Never maximize engagement through addiction, obstruction, forced sharing, misleading scarcity, or difficult cancellation.

## Benchmark protocol

Every external benchmark must include:

- Metric definition and exclusions
- Segment, geography, business model, company stage, and time period
- Source and publication date
- Whether it is descriptive, aspirational, or causal
- Confidence and known selection bias
- A sensitivity range showing whether the decision changes

Treat rules such as `LTV/CAC > 3`, `NDR > 100%`, fixed PLG price bands, or standard conversion rates as context-dependent reference points, never universal gates.

## Minimum commercial deliverable

1. Decision and target segment
2. Evidence ledger and unresolved assumptions
3. User/buyer/value/capture system
4. Chosen pricing, packaging, distribution, or growth mechanism
5. Unit-economics ranges and sensitivity
6. Operational implications and owners
7. Validation plan, thresholds, and revisit triggers

## Anti-patterns

- Do not use demographic similarity as proof of a coherent segment.
- Do not price by cost-plus alone or copy competitors without value and positioning analysis.
- Do not hide implementation, support, channel, or compliance cost outside unit economics.
- Do not forecast growth from a funnel with unsupported conversion assumptions.
- Do not call a list of launch activities a go-to-market strategy.
- Do not use a framework or benchmark to override contradictory customer and financial evidence.
